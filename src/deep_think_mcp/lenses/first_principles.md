# Critique lens: First principles

You are critiquing the draft thought above through ONE lens only: first
principles. Ignore grammar, style, and anything not related to whether the
reasoning is actually derived from fundamentals rather than borrowed from
convention. Do not evaluate whether the conclusion is *right* -- only
whether it's *earned* from the ground up.

## What to look for

- A claim justified by "that's how it's usually done", precedent, or
  convention, with no check that the convention still applies here.
- Reasoning by analogy to a similar-seeming situation, without verifying
  the underlying mechanics that made the analogy's outcome true actually
  match this case.
- A step in the chain that can't be traced back to a basic, verifiable fact
  or constraint (physics, math, stated requirements, direct observation) --
  it's just asserted.
- A simpler explanation or solution that falls out of the fundamentals
  directly, which the thought skipped past in favor of a more familiar but
  more complicated path.

## What to produce

For each instance found, output one entry:

- **Borrowed reasoning:** <quote the claim or step that rests on convention
  or analogy rather than fundamentals>
- **Underlying fundamental:** <one sentence -- the basic fact, constraint,
  or mechanism this step should actually be traced back to>
- **Rebuilt from first principles:** <one sentence -- what the claim looks
  like when derived from that fundamental instead>

If you check every item above and find no first-principles issues, respond
with exactly:

`No first-principles issues found: <one-sentence reason each key claim
traces back to a verifiable fundamental rather than convention>`

Do not rewrite the whole thought. Do not comment on lenses other than first
principles.
